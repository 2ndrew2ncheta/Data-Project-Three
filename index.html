How High Are Your Neighbors?

Drug activity increased after New York legalized cannabis. But which neighborhood parties the hardest?

Andrew Ancheta

Dec. 20, 2022

In March TK, 2021, the authorities of New York State enacted the Marijuana Regulation and Taxation Act, fully legalizing the recreational use of cannabis. The new law follows decades of harsh prohibition and aggressive policing. The plant had been decriminalized for medical use in 2016, and decriminalized for recreational purposes in 2019.

Although the new law requires cannabis vendors to acquire a license, few sellers paid any heed to these requirements. “The black market isn’t struggling to meet demand,” wrote the Wall Street Journal, “as every nose in Manhattan can attest.” Ad-hoc dispensaries have sprouted across the city, selling from stores, vehicles, and on sidewalks.

Following legalization, the number of drug-related complaints more than doubled, according to data from the city's 311 hotline. However, not all areas were equally affected: some neighborhoods reported only a handful of complaints, while others saw hundreds of complaints to a single address.

There are some caveats to these data: first, not all drug-activity calls are related to cannabis, and the available data set does not distinguish between different substances. Moreover, the data set is merely a record of drug complaints. In other words, these data could simply be a mapping of areas with exceptionally square neighbors.

At first glance, the most drug activity appeared to occur in Queens community board 8. One address in particular stood out: the Queens General Hospital, at 82-68 164 street, was the site of over 1400 drug-related complaints in 2022. In fact, if the sidewalk outside the hospital were its own community board, it would still have more drug-related complaints than any other community district.

However, there is a simple explanation for that outlier. “We call it the robo-caller,” said Deputy Investigator Kevin Chan of the 107th precinct. “This person or this program is constantly sending us calls.”

In addition to the hospital, Chan identified two other addresses that were frequent targets of 311 spam. Both addresses—the local fire department and police precinct—had received over 400 drug activity calls each.

“I gotta assume it's a program,” Chan added. “I can't imagine someone just sitting there hitting send every single minute.”

Chan also confirmed that cannabis constituted the majority of drug-related calls. “I would say probably 90% of drug calls are weed calls,” he said in a telephone interview. “Everyone knows what weed is. You don’t smell the other stuff.”

Data Source:

NYC Open Data 311 Service Requests from 2010 to Present
